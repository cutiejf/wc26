Welcome to the 2026 FIFA World Cup™
2026 Edition  •  3 Host Nations  •  16 Cities  •  48 Teams  •  12 Groups  •  1 Champion

From the opening whistle at the legendary Estadio Azteca to the crowning of a champion at MetLife Stadium in New York/New Jersey, every moment is set to make history.

This programme is your complete guide: live scores, schedules, standings, venue details and the full knockout bracket — everything you need, all in one place.

FIFA World Cup 26™ — Rules & Operations
General Match Laws
  • Standard Duration: Each match lasts 90 minutes, consisting of two 45-minute periods.
  • IFAB Standards: All matches are played in accordance with the Laws of the Game as in force for the tournament.
  • Technology: Goal-Line Technology (GLT) verifies all goal decisions. VAR and semi-automated offside technology are used for all match-changing incidents.
  • Official Language: In case of any discrepancy in match laws, the English version is authoritative.
  • Full Laws: The official IFAB Laws of the Game — view the current edition here.
Substitution Rules
  • Standard Allotment: Maximum of 5 substitutes per match.
  • Opportunities: Maximum of 3 substitution opportunities during regulation time (excluding half-time).
  • Half-Time: Substitutions made during the half-time interval do not count as a used opportunity.
  • Extra Time: One additional substitute granted (total 6) with one additional opportunity. Substitutions before extra time or at ET half-time do not count.
  • Concussion Subs: One additional permanent concussion substitution permitted per match. The opposing team receives one additional regular substitution in return.
26Rules
FIFA World Cup 26™ — Rules & Operations
Disciplinary: Cards & Suspensions
  • Yellow Cards: Two cautions in two different matches results in automatic suspension from the next match.
  • Card Cancellation: Single yellow cards are cancelled after the quarter-finals to ensure no player misses the final due to a semi-final caution.
  • Red Cards: A direct or indirect red card results in automatic suspension from the next match. FIFA may impose additional sanctions.
  • Carry-over: Pending red card suspensions carry over to the final tournament. Single yellows from qualifiers do not carry over.
  • VAR: VAR may only be consulted for goals, penalties, direct red cards, and mistaken identity, during the events of clear and obvious errors or serious missed incidents.
Extra Time & Penalties (Knockout Stages)
  • Structure: Two 15-minute periods.
  • Intervals: 5-minute break after regulation time. Short drinks break (max 1 min) permitted at ET half-time.
  • The Coin Toss: First toss determines which goal kicks are taken at. Second toss winner chooses whether to take the first or second kick.
  • Player Position: During the interval before penalties, all players must remain on the pitch, then in the centre circle for the duration of kicks.
  • Cards Reset: All yellow cards are cleared at the start of the penalty shoot-out. Note: two yellows during the shoot-out still result in a red card and dismissal; any card shown also counts toward cumulative suspension thresholds.
27Rules
FIFA World Cup 26™ — Rules & Operations
Kits, Numbers, & Prohibitions
  • Numbers: Only 1 to 26 are permitted. Number 1 is reserved exclusively for a goalkeeper.
  • Display: Numbers must be clearly visible on the chest, back of shirt, and front of shorts.
  • Names: Last name or popular name must appear above the number and match the official release list.
  • No Messages: Political, religious, or personal messages or slogans are strictly prohibited on all playing equipment and undergarments.
  • Spare Kits: Teams must provide goalkeeper shirts without names or numbers for outfield players who may need to take the goalkeeper position.
  • Kit Color Allocation: FIFA informs teams of colors no later than one month before the competition. Priority order: Team A playing kit, Team B playing kit, Team A goalkeeper kit, Team B goalkeeper kit, match officials’ kit.
Group Stage Tie-Breakers
  • If teams are level on points, ranking is determined by:

    1. H2H Points: Points earned versus tied teams only.

    2. H2H Goal Difference: Goals for minus against versus tied teams.

    3. H2H Goals Scored: Total goals scored versus tied teams.

    4. Overall Goal Difference: Goals for minus against in group.

    5. Overall Goals Scored: Total goals scored in group.

    6. Fair Play Score: Deducts points for cautions and expulsions.
    Yellow −1 | Indirect Red −3 | Direct Red −4 | Yellow + Direct Red −5

    e.g. Teams tied on all metrics: A has 1 direct red + 2 yellows (−6), B has 1 yellow + 1 indirect red (−4). B advances with the better score.

    7. FIFA World Ranking — to view complete rankings, click here.

FIFA World Cup 26™ — Rules & Operations
Other Equipment
  • Sleeve Badges: FIFA supplies players’ sleeve badges with the official competition logo (right sleeve).
  • Team Equipment: FIFA provides all drinks bottles, coolers, medical bags, etc. Only these may be used at stadiums and training sessions.
  • Captain’s Armband: The captain must wear the FIFA-issued armband that most clearly contrasts with the sleeve. Non-compliance may lead to disciplinary proceedings.
  • Footballs: FIFA exclusively supplies and selects all footballs. Only these balls may be used for official training and warm-ups.
  • Warm-up Bibs: Only FIFA-provided warm-up bibs allowed during training, pre-match warm-ups, on benches, and for substitutes.
Match Day Operations
  • Arrival: Teams must arrive at the stadium 90 minutes before kick-off. Each must reach host country 5 days before their first match.
  • Start List: Submitted 90 minutes before kick-off.
  • The Bench: Maximum of 26 people (15 substitutes and 11 officials, including one team doctor).
  • Warm-ups: A maximum of 5 players and 1 official may warm up simultaneously. Use of balls is prohibited during these warm-ups.

    Note: Pertains to off-pitch action while the match is in play. A designated zone to partake will be established to both teams prior to match.

  • Prohibitions: No smoking or vaping in the technical area. In stadiums, only permitted in designated areas.
29Rules
FIFA World Cup 26™ — Rules & Operations
Stadiums & Field of Play
  • Pitch Surface: Matches must be on natural grass. No non-football events 2 months prior & no events of any kind 1 month prior.
  • Dimensions: Game pitch shall be 105m × 68m.
  • Maintenance: FIFA defines grass heights and watering schedule.
  • Equipment: Goals must have white posts and nets with dark supports and padding. Spare goals, nets, and corner flags must be stored nearby.
  • Retractable Roofs: FIFA decides if the roof is open or closed. If weather worsens, the referee may order it closed for the duration of the match. The roof must only be moved when the pitch is empty.
  • Cooling Break: In the event of extreme heat, a cooling break of 90 seconds to no more than 3 minutes is permitted.
Special Circumstances
  • Abandoned Matches: If abandoned, the match resumes at the exact minute and scoreline with original players. Teams use remaining substitutions; sent-off players cannot be replaced. Play restarts at the last decision (i.e. goal kick), or a dropped ball from the point of halt.
  • Goalkeeper Replacement: Any incoming replacement goalkeeper shall be assigned the next possible unused shirt number (e.g., 27).
  • Withdrawals & Exclusions:If a team withdraws or is excluded during the group stage, all their results are declared null and void. FIFA retains sole discretion over replacements and further actions.
  • Rescheduling: FIFA has right to cancel, reschedule, or relocate matches for any reason. Includes health, safety, security, or force majeure.

History
The North American Connection

Since the first whistle blew in Uruguay in 1930, the FIFA World Cup™ has evolved from a 13-team invitational into a global phenomenon. In 2026, the tournament marks its most ambitious chapter yet. For the first time in history, the world’s stage expands to 48 nations, bringing 104 matches to 16 iconic host cities across 3 great nations: Canada, Mexico, and the United States.

No Qualifiers Needed The 1930 tournament is the only one held without qualification — all nations were simply invited.
Two at Once The first two World Cup matches kicked off simultaneously on July 13, 1930: France beat Mexico 4–1 and the United States beat Belgium 3–0. French striker Lucien Laurent scored the very first goal in World Cup history.
A Legacy Reimagined Following in the footsteps of legends from Pelé to Messi, a new generation of icons will compete in an expanded format featuring a brand-new Round of 32.
A Continent United From the high-altitude history of Mexico City’s Estadio Azteca—the first stadium to host three World Cups—to the first-ever men’s World Cup matches on Canadian soil in Toronto and Vancouver, and the final showdown in New York New Jersey.
Canada’s First Gold Galt FC — nicknamed “The Galt Porridge Eating Invincibles” — dominated Ontario Cup in 1901–03 & represented Canada at the 1904 St. Louis Olympics, winning gold in the first-ever international soccer tournament on North American soil.

36History
Records — Team
World Cup Titles

Brazil hold the record with 5 titles. The last six tournaments (2002–2022) produced six different champions — Brazil, Italy, Spain, Germany, France, Argentina — the longest-ever streak of unique winners.

World Cup Finals

Germany have appeared in the most finals (8) and suffered the most losses there. Netherlands hold the record for most finals without a title (3). England, Spain, and Uruguay have never lost a final. Since 1998, France have reached 4 finals in 8 tournaments — the most of any nation in that period. Europe leads overall: 12 titles from 29 final appearances; South America: 10 titles from 15.

Defending Champions

Five defending champions have been eliminated at the group stage: Italy (1950), Brazil (1966), France (2002), Italy (2010), Spain (2014), and Germany (2018). Italy hold the record for the longest gap between titles: 44 years (1938–1982).

Attendance

Highest single-match attendance: Uruguay v Brazil, 1950 — 173,850 at the Maracanã. Highest tournament total: 3,587,538 at USA 1994 — still the record despite that being a 24-team format. Every tournament since has had 32 teams and more matches, yet none have matched it.

Player Awards

Lionel Messi holds the record for most Player of the Match awards: 11 overall, 5 in a single tournament, across 4 tournaments — and is the only two-time Golden Ball winner. Cristiano Ronaldo also earned POTM honours at 4 tournaments. Brazil lead nations with 25 POTM wins across 16 players. No player has ever won the Golden Boot twice. Africa, Asia, and Oceania have yet to produce a Young Player Award winner.

Records — Goalkeeping & Cards
Goalkeeping

No goalkeeper has won the Golden Glove twice. Four ‘keepers share the record for most clean sheets in a tournament (5). Most clean sheets (career): Peter Shilton & Fabien Barthez — 10 each. Longest shutout run: Walter Zenga (Italy, 1990) — 517 consecutive minutes. Most goals conceded: Antonio Carbajal & Mohamed Al-Deayea — 25 each.

Card Records

Most yellows (team): Argentina — 123. Most reds (team): Brazil — 11. Most reds (individual): Rigobert Song & Zinedine Zidane — 2 each. Most cards (individual): Javier Mascherano — 7 yellows across 4 tournaments. Most reds (referee): Arturo Brizio Carter (Mexico) — 7 reds in 5 matches. Most cards in a final: Spain v Netherlands, 2010 — 15 (14Y, 1R). First red in a final: Pedro Monzon (Argentina), 65’ vs West Germany, 1990.

Fair Play Award

First given at Mexico 1970; Brazil have won it most — 3 outright plus 1 shared with Spain (2006).

In History

The 1942 and 1946 editions are the only World Cups ever cancelled — both lost to World War II — in the tournament’s 96-year history.

Did You Know?
Three Yellow Cards

During Australia v Croatia at the 2006 World Cup, Croatian defender Josip Šimuï¬&nic; was shown three yellow cards in a single match before the referee realised his error and showed a red. He remains the only player in World Cup history to receive three yellows in one game.

Eliminated & Unbeaten

Switzerland at the 2006 World Cup became the first — and to date only — team eliminated from a World Cup without conceding a single goal. They went out on penalties to Ukraine after a 0–0 draw, losing 3–0 in the shootout.

The Trophy & the Dog

The Jules Rimet Trophy was stolen in London in 1966, weeks before the tournament. It was recovered after a failed ransom attempt — found wrapped in newspaper in a suburban garden by a dog named Pickles. The replacement trophy was stolen in Brazil in 1983 and has never been recovered.

Corner Flags & the Final

The 1974 World Cup Final was delayed by 10 minutes because construction workers forgot to install the corner flags. FIFA’s Laws of the Game explicitly require them, making it one of the strictest criteria for a match to begin.

40Did You Know?
Did You Know?
Battle of Nuremberg

The Portugal v Netherlands match at the 2006 World Cup is the most carded game in history: 4 red cards and 16 yellows. Referee Valentin Ivanov showed 20 cards in total — a record that still stands.

Most Yellows in a Single Game

Argentina v Netherlands in the 2022 quarter-final holds the record for the most yellow cards shown in a single match: 18 yellows — 10 to the Netherlands and 8 to Argentina.

Three Generations, One Name

Three generations of the Weiss family — grandfather, father, and son — all named Vladimir Weiss, all played international football for Czechoslovakia or Slovakia. One name, one sport, three eras.

Ireland’s Unbeaten Exit & India’s No-Show

Ireland reached the Italia ’90 quarter-finals without winning a single game in 90 minutes. India qualified for 1950 after all opponents withdrew — then pulled out themselves.

The Lost Hat-Trick

American Bert Patenaude scored the first World Cup hat-trick in 1930, but FIFA’s records misattributed one goal to a teammate for over 70 years — crediting Argentina’s Guillermo Stabile instead. It was only officially corrected in 2006, more than 30 years after Patenaude died.
